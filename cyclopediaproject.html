Cyclopedia

Overview

Our group, In Control, Totally, have decided to continue work on producing Cyclopedia as our project. Cyclopedia is a platform, supported by three applications, that allows cyclists to plan their route with real-time, crowdsourced reporting of obstructions and route conditions. This will aid cyclists in deciding on a safe and comfortable route, with the aim to assist the increased uptake of the commuter cycling and provide local governments (and other responsible organisations) an improved source of information to be utilised in undertaking maintenance and new developments.

The platform will include options to search for cycling routes and view the real-time comfort and safety information for various routes. The goal is to not only improve the quality of information accessible by cyclists and organisations, but to help reduce carbon emissions and save the community money by sponsoring the increasing uptake of commuter cycling throughout Australia.

The motivation for producing a product like Cyclopedia stems from the lack of high-quality information available to those who simply want to avoid Australia’s current top transportation method – driving a motor vehicle. That is, there is an ever-increasing number of high-quality sources of real-time information for motorists, including both public and private platforms such as Live Traffic NSW, Waze, Plugshare, and more – however, switching to riding a bike a noticeable difference lies in the lack of up-to-date information. With the popularity of such applications when driving, we believe there is significant demand for a platform that mostly copies the concept of crowdsourced route information and implements it in a way that is designed for cyclists first and foremost.

As mentioned, there are several services and tools on the market that are utilised in a similar fashion to our intentions for Cyclopedia. These include services such as Snap Send Solve, a simple maintenance reporting app. There are also options such as Bikemap, Strava, and Citymapper which have mapping and/or navigation capabilities. However, our project is unique in that it is a platform built specifically with cyclists in mind and not only includes maps and the ability to report issues, but the ability to rate cycling routes on other (cycling specific) metrics, as well as other features specifically designed to help the cycling community, all in one convenient application.

Scope and Limits

To enable the application to be produced at a steady but satisfactory pace, the project will be built in stages. Initially, planning and production will only consist of the minimum viable product (MVP) which will also be referenced as Stage 1.

Stage 1 of the Cyclopedia application will primarily consist of four major components: The backend, to be developed on the Google Cloud Platform to allow simple multi-platform access; The iOS mobile application, to be developed as a frontend; The Android mobile application, also to be developed as a frontend, and; The web application, developed as a frontend to be designed as a ‘dashboard’ for registered users to access and analyse reported data. Stage 1 will also include several minor components that aim to enhance the user experience, or allow users to fix problems without needing to contact us. These include:

  • A social media presence, including YouTube, Instagram and Twitter, to:
      • Advertise the product
      Engage with the communities we operate in
      Advocate for better infrastructure
      Demonstrate the positives of cycling as a means of transportation
      Teach the communities we operate in how to use cycling as a means of transportation
      Demonstrate how the application is to be utilised
  • A website, including information such as:
      • Showcasing product features
      Contact information
      Instructions on how to access data collected
      User support, including a knowledge base/instruction manual
  • A forum, for use by the communities we operate in for general discussion around cycling, as well as user support and feedback
  • A Customer Relationship Management (CRM) utility, for storing subscriber contact and sales information going forward

As the size of the database held will initially be too small to effectively sell to organisations, efforts will be prioritised in building a user base. The product will initially be completely free for all – the web application will only be available to registered users, primarily staff, and will not have any use until it is decided to begin selling the data obtained to organisations. As such, no payment processing will be required – however, billing provisions should be made.


Mobile Applications

The iOS application, as well as the Android application, will be a primary method of data input and provide utility to the user by accessing the main database stored in the backend and displaying the data:

  • Data must be searchable and returned from the backend in a user-friendly manner
  • Data must be viewable on a map, or in a list

Both mobile applications will be free to obtain from each platform’s respective application repository, and will not include advertising, at least initially. The application’s design should include provisioning for un-obtrusive banner advertising to be implemented in the future.

mobile applications must adhere to their respective platforms’ user interface design guidelines to the best of our ability. Ensuring adherence to these guidelines will result in a cohesive user experience on either platform, whilst maintaining similar usage patterns should a user switch between the applications. The applications must be designed in such a way that no tutorial explaining the interface is required – although, a knowledge base will still be provided on our website to assist users if need be.

Both mobile applications must be capable of taking reports from the user in a manner that does not require further explanation. Location inputs should be verified against the device’s current GPS position, to prevent inaccurate reports from being made. Users must only be allowed to submit images and text created at the time of input. Users must be offered the opportunity to submit as much information as possible, as dictated by the database in use for the location provided.

Both mobile applications must allow users the opportunity to create an account with us using either Apple ID or Google Account single sign-on (SSO). Account information is to be received as dictated by the backend’s requirements, and stored in the relevant database meeting all Australian security and privacy regulations. Accounts need not be created to submit a report – inputs can be made anonymously if the user desires.

Stage 1 mobile applications will not include any form of point-to-point navigation at this time, although provisions should be made to allow this feature to be added shortly after release. Both mobile applications should make use of modern development technologies, including Swift and Java programming languages, and Xcode or Android Studio IDEs respectively.


Web Application

Although not intended to be utilised in full as part of Stage 1, the web application will be a key component of the project, as it will be the primary means subscribers (organisations) will access, analyse and export the data collected by users through the mobile applications.

Similar to the mobile applications, the web application will provide utility to subscribers by accessing the main database stored in the backend and displaying the data:

  • Data must be searchable and returned from the backend in a user-friendly manner
  • Data must be viewable on a map, or in a list

Further to this, the web application must be capable of providing overviews for each type of data stored, as dictated by the backend. For example, a subscriber must be able to view the number of reports received in a chosen geographical area on a graph, presented by months of the year. The exact options to be made available are yet to be determined.

The web application must follow a similar user interface design to the Android application, and allow subscribers to not only access the data stored but also manage their user account. Unlike the mobile applications, the web application will not make use of SSO at this stage, nor will subscribers be able to manage organisation accounts for use by multiple staff. These are features that would be considered shortly after release, so provisions should be made in development. The web application must make use of modern web technologies such as HTML5, CSS3, JavaScript, PHP and MySQL.

Backend – Data Storage

The backend will primarily serve as a means of storing the immense amount of data expected to be collected by the mobile applications, as well as personal information collected to maintain user accounts. It has been decided the backend will be based on the Google Cloud Platform, meaning all infrastructure maintenance is largely outsourced and can match demand at any given moment.

For Stage 1 of the project, all data will be stored locally in Sydney or Melbourne if available. All data must be stored under Australian security and privacy regulations, as appropriate. Initially, it is expected three databases will be required to make the product operational, however, the expectation should be that more databases will be necessary. These three databases and some of their core values are outlined below, subject to change:

  • User Accounts
      • First name, last name, mobile number, email address, date of birth, residential postcode.
      Passwords (or similar) if required should not be stored as plaintext – an investigation into implementation required.
  • Subscriber accounts
      • First name, last name, organisation name, mobile number, email address, postal address, billing address.
      Passwords should not be stored as plaintext – an investigation into implementation required.
  • City of Sydney data (subject to change)
      • Hidden values include:
           Unique identifier
           Submission time
           Updated time
           Time last refreshed
           User information where appropriate
      Values submitted by users but only accessible to subscribers:
           Priority requested
           Weather
           Comments intended for the organisation responsible
           Contact details where appropriate
      Values submitted by users requiring staff review:
           Location information – street, suburb, postcode
           The direction of travel (up to two selections) – N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW
           Type of route – options to be based on local government area/state design guidelines, some with different grades of perceived safety (for example, busy      arterial road vs quiet residential street)
      Values submitted by users presented as an average score:
           Perceived safety (out of 5)
           Route ride quality (out of 2)
      Values submitted by users:
           Report type – check-in, compliment, complaint, maintenance request, community caution
           Imagery
           General comments

No group members are familiar with the technologies needed to develop the backend at this stage – as such, further investigation is required.

Tools and Technologies

Several different tools and technologies would be required if we were to take this project to completion.

For project management, we are currently using Notion and Microsoft Teams along with the Microsoft Office 365 tools. For prototyping we have found Figma to be a very useful tool and would likely continue to use this in the future. If we were to continue using these tools, it would incur costs as follows: Notion: $8 per user per month and Figma: $12 per user per month (although free for students and educators). As our team is not fully satisfied with the user experience in Microsoft 365, we would be considering using an alternative such as Slack ($6.67 per user per month) or Zoom ($17.50 - $23 per user per month, depending on the plan).

For practical ease of development and convenience, many of the tools that we plan to use are from Google. For the backend cloud storage, we plan to use Google Cloud Platform. The pricing of this is dependent on usage and other specifications. For example, a single 30GiB Cloud SQL Database (MySQL or PostgreSQL) costs USD $42.09 per month (with a 1-year commitment) (Google Cloud n.d.).

For the Android and iOS apps, there are a number of tools we could use for both. These include OAuth (‘sign in with Google/Facebook etc.’) for fast and secure authentication, and possibly also Stripe (or a similar tool) for processing payments. We would also need some good ways to test the application. This could be a specific testing tool like LambdaTest ( USD $30+/month for 2+ parallel tests) (LambdaTest n.d.), or physical devices (which would also incur a large cost), possibly also in conjunction with Vysor (Enterprise license is USD $2/user/month) (Vysor n.d.).

The Google Maps API will most likely be used for the Android application. The Google Maps API is free to use up to 30,000 requests per month (Google Maps Platform n.d.) and has many options for native integration with Android apps using the Maps SDK for Android, and the Android Studio IDE. The Kotlin and Java programming languages would be used, as they are the standard for Android programming. Google Play Console registration costs $25, and is required to publish Android apps.

For the iOS app, Apple’s Mapkit is a likely candidate for a map API. It has many features, and supports native integration with iOS apps (Apple Developer n.d. a). Developing the iOS application will take place using the XCode IDE, which is Apple’s primary development tool (Apple Developer n.d. b). The Swift programming language would be used, being the primary programming language for iPhone app development. An Apple Mac laptop is required for this. Based on a MacBook Air M1, these would cost $1500 each (Apple n.d.). In addition, the Apple Developer program needs to be enrolled in in order to publish iOS apps. This incurs a cost of USD $99 per year, or $299 a year for the enterprise program (Apple Developer n.d. c).

For the web application, a map API would need to be used, likely Google Maps or Apple MapKit as we are already using it for the respective smartphone apps. The development of the Website would likely be done using an IDE such as VS Code, and programming languages such HTML, CSS, JavaScript, PHP (or another server-side language or framework such as Node.js), possibly with other web frameworks and libraries such as jQuery, React, or Vue.js.

Our team has very limited experience with any of the above tools and technologies. None of us have ever used a Map API, or developed an Android or iOS application before. Most of us have experience with basic web development, however, and we also have some experience with general programming, for example in Java and Python, and some basic experience using IDEs. In addition, throughout this course we have gained experience using Figma, which we used as our primary prototyping tool, Notion, and Microsoft Teams and Office 365, which we have used extensively for collaboration on our work.

Testing

Cyclopedia will be designed with UCD principles in mind and will be tested within the group throughout the various phases of the development process from showing and approving of low fidelity wireframes to high fidelity prototypes. Once finished or in the final stages of development, The Guerrilla user testing method deployed in a popular cycling path would be best suited for testing the app as it will best capture an authentic user experience from Cyclopedia’s primary user group [cyclists], however, if the testing method is not applicable employing Guerrilla testing tactics on friends and family and gauging their interactions and thoughts on it as a user would suffice as Cyclopedia will have an intuitive interface and its function can easily be understood by non-cyclists
